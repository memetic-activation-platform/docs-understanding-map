    Reframing value for regenerative coordination¶

    TL;DR¶

    I'm not aiming this document at

    • Deliberate Lineage Placement ‚Äî I'm primarily aiming this document at a Horizon 3 audience and want to firmly position the MAP within the historical and ongoing evolution of the concept of capital, from classical economics through natural capital, multi-capital frameworks, and Context-Based Sustainability, making it clear the platform is part of this larger intellectual and ethical progression.
    • Shift from Utilitarian ‚ÄúResource‚Äù Framing ‚Äî Moves away from the extractive, instrumental connotations of resource, which implies value only in terms of human use, toward a framing that honors intrinsic, relational, and regenerative value.
    • Plurality of Value Forms ‚Äî The plural form capitals emphasizes that there are multiple distinct and non-interchangeable forms of value (e.g., natural, social, memetic, experiential), each with its own thresholds, dynamics, and stewardship requirements.
    • Life-Centric Emphasis via ‚ÄúVital‚Äù ‚Äî Draws on the etymology of vital (‚Äúof or pertaining to life‚Äù) to signal that these capitals are life-sustaining, contextually sacred, and essential to the flourishing of living systems.
    • Governance and Stewardship Alignment ‚Äî Supports MAP‚Äôs consent-based, threshold-aware governance model, in which capital flows are tracked, balanced, and regenerated in relationship, rather than accumulated or exploited.

    üõë From ‚ÄúResources‚Äù to Relationship¶

    In the early phases of MAP design, I used the term "resource" to refer to things that could be offered, exchanged, or accessed within the network: data, knowledge, care, presence, tools, energy, space, and more.

    But I was never comfortable with the term.

    "Resource" implies a utilitarian, extractive view ‚Äî that value lies in how something can be used. This framing, inherited from industrial and colonial paradigms, obscures the intrinsic worth and relational significance of what is being referenced.

    A redwood is not just lumber.
    A grandmother is not just a care resource.
    A language is not just a tool for communication.

    Each of these is a living carrier of value ‚Äî embedded in ecological, cultural, and spiritual contexts.

    We needed a term that honored that complexity. A term that invited stewardship, not extraction.

    üí° Why ‚ÄúCapital‚Äù?¶

    Despite its baggage, capital has undergone significant evolution over the past century ‚Äî from its origins in classical economics to frameworks that recognize social, natural, and cultural capital. The term today can mean:

    • A stock of trust, knowledge, or care
    • A regenerative capacity
    • A foundational ingredient for thriving

    By reclaiming and pluralizing the term ‚Äî Vital Capitals ‚Äî we acknowledge this evolution, while clearly departing from narrow financial connotations.

    üåø Why the Plural: "Vital Capitals"?¶

    Pluralizing ‚Äúcapital‚Äù is not stylistic ‚Äî it is philosophical.

    • It affirms that value is plural: attention is not water, and water is not trust.
    • It guards against reductionism, preventing all forms of value from being flattened into a single metric.
    • It invites holistic awareness: that different capital types flow differently, regenerate differently, and matter differently.

    üå± Why ‚ÄúVital‚Äù?¶

    ‚ÄúVital‚Äù comes from the Latin vitalis, meaning of or pertaining to life (from vita: life). The word has long carried meanings like:

    • Essential to life (as in "vital signs")
    • Life-animating (as in "vital energy")
    • Deeply important (as in "a vital issue")

    To call something vital is to say:
    ‚ÄúThis matters to life.‚Äù

    In the MAP, we use ‚Äúvital‚Äù to signal that these capitals are not optional, extractable resources ‚Äî they are the flows that sustain and regenerate living systems.

    ü™ô 1. Classical Economics: Capital as Reproducible Wealth¶

    (1700s‚Äì1900s)

    Rooted in thinkers like Adam Smith, David Ricardo, and Karl Marx, capital was defined as produced goods used to produce other goods ‚Äî separate from land or labor.

    • Capital = tangible, reproducible, and invested for gain
    • Centered on the logic of ownership and accumulation

    Capital was treated as a store of value and a means of production ‚Äî the engine of industrial growth.

    üå± 2. Natural Capital (1970s‚Äì1990s)¶

    (Herman Daly, Paul Hawken, Amory & Hunter Lovins)

    Introduced the idea that ecosystems and biodiversity are essential stocks of value, supporting all other forms of capital.

    • Included: air, water, land, biodiversity, and ecosystem services
    • Reframed nature as asset, deserving preservation or pricing

    Natural capital made ecology legible to economics ‚Äî but still framed in scarcity and market terms.

    üåÄ 3. Expanded Capital Frameworks (1990s‚Äì2010s)¶

    üìä A. Triple Bottom Line & Multi-Capital Accounting¶

    (People, Planet, Profit ‚Üí Six Capitals)
    Popularized by the International Integrated Reporting Council (IIRC):

    • Financial
    • Manufactured
    • Human
    • Intellectual
    • Social/Relationship
    • Natural

    Valuable for scope, but generally lacked context-aware thresholds or regenerative principles.

    üåæ B. Permaculture‚Äôs 8 Forms of Capital¶

    (Ethan Roland & Gregory Landua, ~2011)

    A broader and more culturally grounded framework:

    • Living, Financial, Material (Built), Intellectual, Experiential, Social, Cultural, Spiritual
    • Framed capital as plural, reciprocal, and qualitative

    ‚ÄúReal wealth is more than money‚Äù ‚Äî the beginning of ecologies of capital.

    üìè 4. Context-Based Sustainability (CBS)¶

    Mark W. McElroy, 2008 ‚Äî origin of the term ‚ÄúVital Capitals‚Äù

    CBS reframed capital through the lens of justice, thresholds, and context.

    • Vital Capitals = the capital stocks stakeholders depend on for well-being
    • Introduced Sustainability Quotient (SQ):
      Actual Impact √∑ Contextual Threshold
    • If SQ > 1 ‚Üí overshoot (unsustainable); if SQ ‚â§ 1 ‚Üí within sustainable bounds

    Capital becomes a relational boundary condition, not just a possession.

    üìå MAP adopts the term ‚ÄúVital Capital‚Äù directly from CBS and extends it holonically.

    üîÅ 5. Regenerative Capital & Post-Growth Economics¶

    (2010s‚Äìpresent)
    Influences: John Fullerton, Kate Raworth, Arthur Brock

    • Wealth reframed as capacity to meet the needs of living systems
    • Regeneration emphasized over extraction
    • Economics as a living system embedded in ecological and cultural contexts

    Capital is now understood as capacity, not asset ‚Äî tracked through flows, not accumulation.
